Ultra-low-power CMOS optical receivers for data centers

Overview

The explosive growth of cloud computing and data centers demands ever-higher bandwidth optical links. This project focuses on designing ultra-high-speed optical receiver front-ends capable of 100+ Gb/s operation while maintaining low power consumption and high sensitivity. These receivers enable the next generation of data center interconnects, supporting the infrastructure behind modern AI workloads and cloud services.

Key Results: 100+ Gb/s throughput, 50% power savings, <1pA input sensitivity

The Challenge

High-speed optical receivers operating beyond 100 Gb/s face fundamental design challenges:

  • Bandwidth vs. Sensitivity Trade-off: Achieving >50 GHz bandwidth while maintaining <1 pA/‚àöHz input-referred noise is extremely difficult
  • Power Consumption: Traditional transimpedance amplifiers (TIAs) consume >100 mW per channel at these speeds
  • CMOS Scaling: Advanced CMOS nodes needed for speed lack the voltage headroom for high gain
  • Parasitics: Package and photodiode capacitance limit bandwidth and introduce peaking
  • Equalization: Chromatic dispersion in long fibers requires complex equalization at the receiver

Solution Approach

Developed an optimized receiver architecture combining innovative circuit techniques with advanced packaging:

Transimpedance Amplifier (TIA) Design

  • Cherry-Hooper Topology: Multi-stage architecture separating transimpedance and voltage gain
  • Shunt-Feedback: Local feedback around input stage for wideband input impedance
  • Active Inductor Peaking: Bandwidth extension without bulky on-chip inductors
  • Adaptive Biasing: Dynamic bias adjustment maintaining constant bandwidth across PVT

Limiting Amplifier (LA)

  • Offset Compensation: DC offset cancellation loop preventing output saturation
  • Automatic Gain Control: Wide dynamic range (>20 dB) with constant output swing
  • Low-Voltage Design: Operates from 1.2V supply in 28nm CMOS
  • 50Œ© Driver: CML output driving transmission lines to CDR or DSP

Co-Design and Integration

  • Photodiode Co-Optimization: Custom germanium-on-silicon photodiode designed for low capacitance
  • Flip-Chip Assembly: Direct die-to-die bonding minimizing parasitic inductance
  • Thermal Management: Integrated temperature sensors and thermal vias
  • ESD Protection: On-chip protection structures with minimal capacitance loading

Circuit Innovations

Bandwidth Extension

  • Active Peaking: Cross-coupled pair creates negative impedance at high frequencies
  • Feed-Forward Equalization: FIR filter compensates for dispersion-induced ISI
  • Group Delay Optimization: All-pass sections flatten group delay across band

Noise Optimization

  • Input Transistor Sizing: Optimal W/L ratio balancing thermal and gate noise
  • Resistor Noise: Minimized feedback resistor value while maintaining stability
  • Common-Mode Rejection: Fully differential topology rejecting supply and substrate noise

Power Reduction Techniques

  • Current Reuse: Stacked transistors sharing bias current
  • Dynamic Biasing: Reduced current during idle periods detected by AGC
  • Clock Gating: Digital control logic powered down when not adjusting AGC

Design Challenges Overcome

Bandwidth Limitations

  • Issue: Photodiode capacitance creates dominant pole at 20 GHz
  • Solution: Inductive peaking and optimized input stage sizing extending bandwidth to 55 GHz

Stability Concerns

  • Issue: Feedback loop instability with high gain and wide bandwidth
  • Solution: Pole-zero cancellation and damping resistors ensuring 60¬∞ phase margin

Power-Bandwidth Trade-off

  • Issue: High bandwidth requires large transconductance, increasing power
  • Solution: Current reuse and optimal transistor sizing achieving 55 GHz at 100 mW
